The Good News vs Pascal's Wager

According to the Christian tradition I grew up in, the Good News (aka, the Gospel) is that Jesus Christ came to Earth to provide us a means of reconciling with God. We were now able to be "saved" from our "sins."

Ironically, that "Good News" turns out to be very bad news for the vast majority of humanity.

To explain this further, let's look at Pascal's Wager1:

God exists God does not exist
You believe heaven nothing
You don't believe hell nothing

In this argument, if you assume God does not exist, the worst thing that will happen to you after you die is nothing at all. And yet, if you assume God does exist, the worst thing that could happen is eternal damnation (hell). Therefore, it is more prudent to believe than to not.

So, there's a lot wrong with this whole thing, but let's ignore all of that for now and just assume Pascal was correct. What does this line of reasoning imply about the character of God and the "Good News"?

Well, if you believe the fundamentalists, the number of people who truly believe is vanishingly small. This means only a tiny portion of humanity will enter into heaven, while the vast majority will be condemned to hell. That sounds much more like bad news to most people. In this case, it would actually be more "good" if God didn't exist, right? If the choice is between people dying and nothing happening, vs them dying, and then having to live for all of eternity in conscious torment, then the former is clearly more "good."

One could argue about individual choice vs God's elect, and what it is any of us truly "deserve," but I hope that it is clear something redemptive would be more "good" rather than something purely punitive.

Perhaps we should rethink what we mean when we talk about the Good News. It seems like if it isn't actually good for the majority of humanity, then it isn't good at all.
