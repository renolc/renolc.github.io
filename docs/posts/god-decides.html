God Chooses, We Don't

A debate as old as time - Calvinism vs Arminianism. Election vs choice. Sovereignty vs free will.1

I'm going to completely ignore all that.

Don't get me wrong, it's an interesting discussion, philosophically speaking. Theologically though, I think the conversation usually misses the mark.

Continuing from my [[god-defines-goodness|previous post]], I wanted to elaborate on my statement that "God chooses who to save."

When I speak of choice, I am not talking about choosing at the individual level, but at the systemic level. Put another way, not about who but how. And in the choosing of how, the who becomes implied.

According to orthodoxy, God is the final authority. There is no outside influence that is forcing any decision that God makes. Also, there is nothing in existence that God hasn't created or decreed. So, the means by which anyone becomes "saved" is, technically speaking, completely arbitrary.

I mean that in the strictest, technical sense. As already stated, God cannot be held to the whim of anything or anyone else -- including the system of salvation. It's not via some cosmic law or natural force, because those only exist as God designed.

So if God so chose, Jesus didn't have to exist at all. Meaning "accepting Jesus as your personal Lord and Savior" could also be thrown out. God could instead have said that none of humanity will be saved. Or all of it. None of the whole system that Christians spend an inordinate amount of time fighting about has any inherent meaning or necessity, because God could have just as easily declared something else.

And so, to bring it all back to my initial point, the idea of "personal choice" is still a concept that relies on God's whim. If God declared that individual choice rather than election is how the system works, then that is only true because God chose it. So in the end, no matter how you look at it, no matter if you're Calvinist or Arminianist, no matter if you prioritize God's sovereignty or love, it is God who has the real power to choose who is saved, because only God gets to choose how salvation works.

The next question is, so then whose responsibility really is it? If "God is love" like Christians and the Bible claim, then what choice do we think God made?

  1. Despite what the title and article may sound like, I am very much not a Calvinist. My arguments here are purely philosophical in nature, and based on the assumptions you would hear in any typical American evangelical church.↩︎
